MEES & EPC

MEES Band C 2028: What Commercial Landlords Need to Know

15 March 2026 · CrowAgent Research Team · 5 min read

The Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards (MEES), introduced under the Energy Efficiency (Private Rented Property) (England and Wales) Regulations 2015 (SI 2015/962), have been tightening steadily. Since 1 April 2023, it has been unlawful to continue letting a commercial property in England and Wales with an Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) rating of F or G. This applied to all existing leases — not just new ones — marking a significant escalation from the original 2018 rules that only covered new lettings.

Now, the UK government has signalled its intention to raise the MEES threshold to EPC Band C by 2028 for commercial properties. While the final statutory instrument has not yet been laid before Parliament, the direction of travel is clear and consistent with the government's broader net zero strategy. The proposed Band C requirement could affect more than 400,000 commercial properties across England and Wales that currently hold an EPC rating of D or E.

What the Band C Threshold Means in Practice

Moving from the current Band E minimum to Band C is not a minor adjustment. Many commercial buildings — particularly older office stock, retail units, and industrial premises built before 2000 — sit at EPC ratings of D or E. Reaching Band C typically requires substantive retrofit interventions: improved insulation, upgraded heating systems, LED lighting, enhanced building management systems, and in some cases window replacements or the installation of renewable energy generation.

For landlords with portfolios of 10 or more commercial properties, the combined cost of reaching Band C across every asset can be substantial. Estimates from industry bodies suggest average costs of £20,000 to £60,000 per property, though older and larger buildings can run significantly higher. The key risk is not just the capital expenditure — it is the possibility that non-compliant properties become unlettable, stranding the asset entirely.

Penalties for Non-Compliance

Under the existing MEES enforcement framework, penalties for non-compliance are calculated based on the rateable value of the property and the duration of the breach. For commercial properties, fines range from £5,000 to £150,000 depending on the length of the breach and the rateable value. Local authority trading standards officers are the enforcement body, and compliance notices are published on a public register — creating both financial and reputational risk.

Regulatory reference: The Energy Efficiency (Private Rented Property) (England and Wales) Regulations 2015 (SI 2015/962), as amended. The Band C proposal builds on the existing MEES framework and is expected to follow the same enforcement structure.
